Dylan Permuy

Dylan Thomas Permuy (born December 14, 1998) is an American photographer known for his work with black and white film. His career is deeply rooted in the tradition of analog photography, and he has garnered a reputation for his mastery of darkroom printing techniques. Dylan is currently immersed in a personal project that delves into the intricate dynamics of family and identity. This ongoing work questions how familial bonds and environmental factors converge to shape individual identity. The project, characterized by its intimate narrative and reflective imagery, stands as a testament to his belief in the power of photography to document and interrogate the human experience.

Early life and education [edit]

Dylan Permuy was born on December 14, 1998, in South Jersey, to Linda Permuy and Edward Permuy. He received his first camera, a Canon Powershot A540, as a gift from his uncle one birthday.

Permuy attended Temple University, where he focused on photography and visual design, before dropping out in 2018. He was living and making work in Philadelphia, PA until moving to New York City in 2021.

Career [edit]

Early career (2009–2014) [edit]

After graduating from NYU, Permuy initially worked as a freelance photographer for several magazines including Dazed, i-D, and The New Yorker. While building his commercial portfolio, he simultaneously developed personal projects that would later form his artistic signature.

In 2012, Permuy held his first solo exhibition, Transient, at the Aperture Gallery in New York, featuring a series of environmental portraits documenting individuals in transition. The exhibition received favorable reviews, with art critic Susan Bright noting his "remarkable ability to capture authentic moments of vulnerability within constructed settings."[2]

During this period, Permuy also collaborated with environmental organizations, creating documentary photography that highlighted climate change impacts in coastal communities. This work appeared in the 2013 publication Shorelines: A Visual Testimony, which combined his images with essays by environmental scientists.[3]

Breakthrough and recognition (2015–2019) [edit]

Permuy's major breakthrough came with his 2015 series Beyond Surface, a collection of large-format portraits examining the relationship between people and their living spaces. The project, exhibited at the Whitney Museum of American Art, earned him the International Center of Photography's Emerging Photographer Award and established him as a significant voice in contemporary photography.[4]

In 2018, Permuy released Urban Fragments, a photographic exploration of architectural details and human presence in metropolitan environments. The series was accompanied by a critically acclaimed monograph published by Aperture and led to his inclusion in the Museum of Modern Art's "New Photography" exhibition that same year.[5]

During this period, Permuy's work was acquired by several major institutions, including the San Francisco Museum of Modern Art, the Victoria and Albert Museum in London, and the Centre Pompidou in Paris.

Recent work (2020–present) [edit]

Since 2020, Permuy has increasingly explored digital and mixed-media approaches to photography. His 2022 series Liminal Spaces combined traditional photography with augmented reality elements, allowing viewers to interact with the images through a smartphone application. The project debuted at the International Center of Photography and has been praised for its innovative approach to photographic presentation.[6]

In 2023, Permuy was commissioned by the National Portrait Gallery to create a series of portraits of climate scientists and environmental activists for their permanent collection. The ongoing project, titled Guardians, has been described by curator Sarah Greenough as "a powerful visual testament to those working at the forefront of our most pressing environmental challenges."[7]
